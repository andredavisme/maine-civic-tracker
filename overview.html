Maine Civic Tracker

A Civic Accountability Platform for Maine Communities

Proof of Concept  ·  Built with Real Data  ·  Operational Today

The Problem

Your town collects taxes. It receives state and federal grants. It spends that money on projects. But when was the last time you could easily see where every dollar came from, where it went, and whether it actually worked?

Most Mainers can’t answer that — not because the information doesn’t exist, but because it’s buried in PDF budgets, town meeting minutes, and spreadsheets nobody has time to read.

Accountability gaps don’t just waste money. They erode trust, allow mismanagement to go unnoticed, and leave residents feeling powerless.

What Maine Civic Tracker Does

💰

Budget Transparency

See every revenue source — taxes, grants, state aid, bonds — by fiscal year.

🏗️

Fund Allocations

Track which projects got funded, how much was spent, and who is responsible.

📊

Outcome Reporting

Verify that projects produced real results with measurable, cited evidence.

📷

Community Evidence

Residents submit photos and reports. A moderator reviews them before they go public.

Who Can Run This — and How

🏘️

Volunteer Civic Org

A small group of neighbors. One person enters data from public records. One reviews submissions. One keeps the tech running.

~$1/month
🏗️

Municipal Partnership

Town staff provide budget data annually. An independent resident moderates community submissions — keeping it honest.

~$26–$56/month
🗺️

Regional Network

One coordinator, multiple communities, a rotating pool of moderators. Covers a full county or region.

~$135–$265/month
Where humans are always required: Moderation decisions, data sourcing from public records, and dispute resolution. Automation handles routing, storage, display, and notifications — but judgment stays with people.

What Your Community Needs to Get Started

  • One person willing to enter budget data from public records (town reports, FOAA requests)
  • One person willing to review resident submissions fairly and consistently
  • One person to handle the technical side — no servers, no coding required to maintain
  • A commitment to sourcing every record from verifiable public documents
You do not need municipal approval to start.
You do not need a large budget.
You do not need technical expertise beyond basic computer literacy.
